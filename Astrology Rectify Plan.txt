**Phase 1 — Input**
The minimum viable input is city name + calendar date. The agent must immediately resolve ambiguities: cities that have moved historical boundaries, changed names, or sit in complex timezone histories (think pre-1918 Central Europe or colonial-era South America). This data goes into a structured record before anything else runs.

**Phase 2 — Research agent**
This is the most labor-intensive phase and the quality bottleneck for everything downstream. The agent performs layered web searches: biography sources, interviews, Wikipedia (for dates), news archives, and databases like IMDb or sports records for celebrities. The goal is a *timestamped life events table* — not just "got married" but "married on 15 June 1997." Events are classified by astrological weight: major outer-planet events (marriage, death of a parent, career peaks, illness, accidents, relocation) are worth much more than minor ones. The agent should also flag events where dates are uncertain or contradicted across sources, and hold those back for cross-validation rather than using them as training data.

**Phase 3 — Astronomical setup**
The agent geocodes the city to latitude and longitude (crucial for house calculations), then resolves the *historical* timezone — not today's, but the one in effect on the birth date. Local mean time was common before timezone standardization. Tools like AstroDataBank's timezone database or Olson's tzdata handle this. The output is a fixed astronomical reference frame: Julian day number, delta-T correction, geographic coordinates.

**Phase 4 — Candidate chart pool**
A full 24-hour day generates 96 candidate charts at 15-minute intervals (or 288 at 5-minute intervals for the second refinement pass). Each chart is fully calculated: all planetary positions (which barely change over 24h, making them nearly useless as differentiators), house cusps, Ascendant, Midheaven, and derived sensitive points like Vertex, Arabic Parts, and house rulers. The ASC changes sign roughly every 2 hours, so you'll typically have 12 possible rising signs as broad brackets before fine-tuning within each.

**Phase 5 — Multi-technique testing**
This is the computational core. For each life event with a known date, the agent tests every candidate chart using four independent methods simultaneously:

*Transits* check whether slow-moving outer planets (Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, Pluto) were making significant aspects to natal angles (ASC/MC/DSC/IC) or to sensitive natal points on the event date. A transit is "hot" when it's within 1° orb. *Secondary progressions* move each natal planet forward one day per year of life — so a person 35 years old has their chart progressed 35 days from the natal. Progressed Moon to natal angles and progressed angles to natal planets are powerful markers. *Solar arc directions* move every point in the chart forward at the rate of the progressed Sun (~1° per year). Angular hits — when a directed planet reaches within 1° of the natal ASC or MC — time events with remarkable precision. *Annual profections* advance the chart one zodiac sign per year (the "profected Ascendant"), activating whatever planets rule or occupy that sign. The lord of the profected year should be active by transit or progression during major life events.

**Phase 6 — Scoring & ranking**
Each candidate time gets scored per technique. A clean scoring function might award 3 points for an outer-planet transit within 0.5° of an angle, 2 points within 1°, 1 point within 2°; similarly for progressions and solar arcs. Profections add a binary "active lord" flag. The aggregate weighted score across all events and all techniques produces a ranked list. The key signal is *convergence* — a time that scores well across all four methods independently is far more trustworthy than one that scores high on only one.

**Phase 7 — Decision and refinement loop**
If the top-scoring candidate is separated from the second-best by more than ~15% of the total score, and it appears consistently across all techniques, the threshold is met. If two or three candidates cluster close together, the agent narrows the window: it drops to 5-minute intervals within the contested range and re-runs Phase 5 with higher precision (tighter orbs, adding primary directions or tertiary progressions). This loop typically converges in 1–2 iterations. If the events list is sparse, the agent goes back to Phase 2 and searches for more events before narrowing.

**Phase 8 — Cross-validation**
The final candidate is tested against events that were deliberately *held out* of the scoring process — a reserved test set. The agent also checks soft evidence: does the Ascendant sign fit the native's known physical appearance (body type, facial structure as described by contemporaries or visible in photos)? Does the chart temperament match documented personality descriptions? Does the 10th-house lord and MC sign align with their career type? These checks can't confirm a time alone but can disqualify a candidate even if its technical scores are high.

**Phase 9 — Output**
The final report states the rectified time with an uncertainty range (typically ±5–10 minutes), a composite confidence score, and a full evidence ledger: which events activated which points, by which method, with what orb. This makes the result falsifiable and auditable — a future researcher can add new events and re-test the same candidate.

The iterative refinement loop is what distinguishes a rigorous agentic process from a single-pass estimate. The more life events available, the faster convergence occurs and the tighter the final window. With 15–20 well-dated events, a 5-minute precision is realistic. With fewer than 8, you may only narrow to the correct 30-minute window — and the cross-validation phase becomes even more important to avoid false positives from sparse data.

## What the workflow gets right

The core logic is sound. The multi-technique approach is exactly what serious rectifiers do — no single method is reliable alone, and convergence across methods is the real signal. The idea of a weighted scored ledger with a held-out test set is actually more rigorous than most human practitioners manage. And the iterative narrowing loop (broad pass → narrow pass) reflects real practice accurately.

## Where it needs serious revision

**The 5–15 minute interval assumption is naïve for the wrong reasons.**

The workflow treats time as the primary variable, but in practice the *house system* is just as variable and far more contested. A Placidus Ascendant at 23° Scorpio becomes a Whole Sign Ascendant at 24° Scorpio with a completely different set of house placements. Before the agent generates a "candidate pool," it must commit to a house system — and that choice is not neutral. Koch, Placidus, Regiomontanus, Whole Sign, and Equal House will produce different rectified times for the *same native* using the *same events*. A real rectification workflow must either run parallel tracks for multiple house systems and compare which one resolves best, or make a methodologically justified choice upfront and state it explicitly. This is one of the most common sources of false confidence in rectification.

**Primary directions are buried in Phase 7 as a refinement tool. They should be in Phase 5 as a primary method.**

Ptolemaic primary directions — especially to the Ascendant and MC — are the oldest and most precise timing tool in traditional astrology, accurate to within days at the correct birth time. Ignoring them in the main scoring phase and only deploying them as a tiebreaker is like running an election and only checking the mail-in ballots if the in-person count is too close. The workflow should treat primaries as a first-class technique. The Naibod arc (0.9856° per year, or 59'8" per year) or the true solar arc key should be specified — the choice of arc key affects the timing by weeks at middle age and months at old age.

**The event classification is underdeveloped and this is fatal.**

The workflow mentions that outer-planet events are "worth more" but doesn't grapple with *which* natal points those planets need to activate. In rectification, not every conjunction is equal. A Saturn transit to a natal Saturn is a Saturn return — it happens to everyone born in that year, regardless of birth time. It carries zero discriminating power. What matters is transits, directions, and progressions to *time-sensitive points* — meaning the angles (ASC, MC, DSC, IC), and planets that rule the houses governing the event type. For a marriage, you want Venus, the ruler of the 7th, or the 7th cusp itself activated. For a career peak, the MC and its ruler. For death of a parent, the 4th or 10th cusp and their rulers. An agent that doesn't know to look at the *right* point for the *right* event type will score spurious hits and miss genuine ones. This requires an event taxonomy with house correspondence built into the scoring logic.

**The research agent will collect the wrong events if not properly constrained.**

Biographies are full of soft events — "rose to prominence," "experienced a creative peak," "went through a difficult period." These are useless for rectification because they can't be dated precisely. The only events that carry weight are those with a known date to within a week, ideally a day. The agent needs to aggressively filter for hard-dated events: marriage date (not year), accident date, surgery date, death of a close relative (with date), publication date, election win/loss date, arrest date, emigration date. For most celebrities, you can typically recover 8–15 such events from careful research. The workflow should explicitly distinguish between *anchor events* (hard-dated, high-weight) and *soft events* (approximate, low-weight or discarded).

**The Moon is absent and this is a serious gap.**

The progressed Moon moves approximately 13° per month — it changes signs roughly every 2.3 years and changes houses on a schedule entirely dependent on the birth time. The progressed Moon's house ingresses and aspects to natal angles are among the most discriminating signals in rectification because they respond to birth time with much higher sensitivity than outer-planet transits. A 10-minute difference in birth time shifts the progressed Moon's position by about 4–5 minutes of arc over 30 years — detectable, but the Moon's house position will differ noticeably over a 30-minute window. The workflow needs a dedicated lunar strand.

**The cross-validation phase relies on physical appearance, which is tricky and contested.**

The idea of checking Ascendant against physical appearance is traditional but notoriously unreliable in practice, for several reasons. First, the descriptions in classical sources (Ptolemy, Lilly, Morin) were written for Mediterranean populations and map poorly to global diversity. Second, contemporary humans live longer and under very different environmental conditions than classical astrologers observed. Third, photographs introduce lighting, angle, and style artifacts. Physical appearance can serve as a weak *disqualifier* — if you arrive at a Capricorn rising for someone who is visibly very tall, broad-shouldered, and leonine in manner, you have reason to be cautious — but it should never be used as a *confirmer*. The workflow should downweight this check significantly and mark it explicitly as soft evidence.

**The confidence scoring treats all techniques as independent, but they are not.**

Secondary progressions and solar arcs are both derived from the same natal chart and both use the arc of the progressed Sun as their basis. They will tend to agree with each other structurally. Treating them as fully independent techniques and summing their scores overcounts the evidence. A real scoring model needs to account for the fact that progressions and solar arcs are correlated — they're not separate witnesses, they're more like two measurements from the same instrument. The only genuinely independent methods in the pool are primary directions (which use a different mathematical framework entirely), profections (which operate on a different time scale), and transits (which are real-time planetary positions, not derived from the natal chart at all).

**Historical timezone resolution is more complex than the workflow suggests.**

For 20th century celebrities born in politically complex regions — pre-partition India, colonial Africa, wartime Europe, anywhere in the former USSR — the timezone question is not just about daylight saving adjustments. It involves wartime double summer time, politically mandated zone changes, and occasionally the difference between civil time and astronomical local mean time used by the birth registry. A 30-minute error here propagates through the entire rectification as a systematic bias and can produce a convincing-but-wrong result. The agent needs access to a specialist historical timezone database (Astro.com's atlas or the ACS International Atlas) and should flag high-uncertainty cases for manual review rather than silently proceeding.

## What is missing entirely

**The Morin method** — Jean-Baptiste Morin de Villefranche's systematic approach using house rulerships and sign-planet relationships to narrow the Ascendant sign before doing any timing work — is absent. This is one of the most logically rigorous pre-filtering steps in rectification and would dramatically reduce the candidate pool before any computation begins. If you can rule out 8 rising signs on structural grounds (the native's life theme strongly favors a 9th-house stellium, their profession maps unambiguously to a particular MC sign), you only need to work within the 4 plausible rising signs, cutting computation by two-thirds.

**Arabic Parts / Lots** — particularly the Lot of Fortune and the Lot of Spirit — are angle-sensitive points that shift rapidly with birth time and should be included in the scoring matrix.

**Antiscia and contra-antiscia** — mirror points across the solstice axis — are used by traditional practitioners and add discriminating hits at times that appear "quiet" by transit but are actually activated through the mirror degree.

**Rectification from known Ascendant sign** — a separate and more tractable problem. If the native's rising sign is known from physical, psychological, or circumstantial evidence, the problem reduces from 288 candidates to roughly 24 (one hour's worth of times within that sign). The workflow should branch here: full rectification vs. within-sign fine-tuning.

## The honest limitation no workflow can fix

Even a perfect implementation of all of the above will fail for some natives. If a person's documented life is uneventful — no dramatic outer-planet events, no hard-dated crises, no public record — the signal-to-noise ratio is too low to achieve 5-minute precision regardless of method. The workflow should include an explicit uncertainty floor: state clearly when the evidence only supports a 30-minute window, not a 5-minute one, and resist the temptation to report false precision. This is where most automated rectification attempts go wrong — they produce a specific time because the algorithm converges, not because the data actually support that specificity.

### Foundational decisions made before writing a single line of code

**As astrologer:** The very first decision — before any candidate generation — is to run the Morin structural pre-filter. This means analyzing the native's biography for life themes: a person with a visibly 9th-house life (academic, international, philosophical, legal) cannot have a 9th-house stellium buried in the 3rd. A native with an unmistakably 10th-house career arc (political power, public reputation, top institutional position) should have strong angular planets or a prominent MC ruler. This structural reasoning typically eliminates 7–9 of the 12 possible rising signs before a single ephemeris calculation runs. The candidate pool drops from 288 to roughly 72. Everything downstream runs faster and cleaner.

**As developer:** The tech stack is `pyswisseph` for ephemeris calculations (it's a Python binding for the gold-standard Swiss Ephemeris), `FastAPI` for the service layer, `PostgreSQL` for the event store with a normalized schema (entity → events → classifications → date ranges), and an LLM API call (Claude) for the NLP event extraction step. Crucially, the candidate grid is computed as a vectorized numpy array — 288 rows, one per candidate time — with columns for every sensitive point across all 4 house systems. All scoring operations are matrix multiplications against the event table, not loops. This keeps the full pass under a second even for 20 life events × 288 candidates × 5 techniques × 4 house systems = ~115,000 scoring operations.

**As data scientist:** The training corpus is AstroDataBank Rodden Rating AA and A charts only — approximately 2,000 verified celebrity birth times. For each chart in the corpus, I extract a feature vector: the rising sign (12-category), the MC sign (12-category), and the angular distance (in degrees) from each of 10 planets to each of the 4 angles. This gives a 12+12+40 = 64-dimensional feature space per chart. I also extract "life theme" embeddings from each celebrity's biography using a sentence transformer — the intuition being that a Scorpio rising politician with a 10th-house Sun should pattern-match other Scorpio rising public figures in the corpus. A gradient-boosted classifier trained on this combined feature space gives me a prior probability distribution over the 12 rising signs for any new native. This prior does not replace deterministic scoring — it initializes the Bayesian prior so the engine starts informed rather than uniform.

### The loop structure — four nested levels

This is where the three roles truly converge. The system runs four loops at different granularities:

**Loop 0 — Morin pre-filter (runs once)**
Input: biography, known life theme, physical description if available.
Process: LLM-assisted structural analysis maps life themes to probable house configurations and plausible rising sign brackets. The data scientist notes this is a classification task — the output is a probability vector over 12 rising signs, not a hard decision. Signs with probability below a threshold (say 5%) are excluded from the candidate grid entirely.
Output: reduced set of plausible rising signs → generates the candidate pool.

**Loop 1 — Broad scoring pass (runs 1–2 times)**
Input: 72–96 candidate times (post-Morin), full event table with hard-dated anchor events, soft events flagged and downweighted.
Process: For each candidate, compute scores across 5 techniques. As astrologer I insist on the following technique ordering by weight: (1) primary directions to angles, weighted highest because they are the most mathematically precise and the oldest; (2) progressed Moon house ingresses, weighted second because they are exquisitely sensitive to birth time — a 10-minute error shifts the ingress date by roughly 2.5 days; (3) solar arc directions to angles; (4) annual profections (active lord testing); (5) outer-planet transits to angles. As data scientist I flag that techniques 3 and 4 share mathematical structure with each other and partially with technique 1 — I apply a correlation penalty: their combined weight is capped so they don't count as three independent witnesses when they are partially dependent.
Bayesian update: multiply the corpus prior by the likelihood vector from this technique run, normalize to sum to 1. The posterior is now a probability distribution over the 72 candidate times.
Decision gate: if the posterior has a clear mode (top candidate's probability > 3× second candidate's), advance to Loop 2. If not, check whether more events can be extracted (back to research agent) before repeating.

**Loop 2 — Narrow pass (runs 1–2 times)**
Input: top candidate ± 30 minutes, regenerated at 1-minute resolution (60 candidates), tighter orbs (0.5° rather than 1°), including techniques not used in Loop 1 (antiscia, Arabic Lots, primary directions under alternative arc keys).
As astrologer: this is where I check the progressed Moon's house position against documented emotional events. A clearly documented period of isolation or chronic illness should coincide with the progressed Moon in the 12th. A marriage period should show the progressed Moon in the 7th or aspecting Venus tightly. These checks are not scored — they are binary pass/fail filters.
As data scientist: at this resolution I also run a bootstrap sensitivity analysis — perturb each scored event's date by ±3 days, re-run scoring, measure how much the posterior mode shifts. A robust rectification produces a stable mode even under this perturbation. An unstable one (where the mode jumps 20+ minutes when one event date is slightly moved) signals that the convergence is spurious — driven by one event, not by consensus.
Output: top candidate narrowed to ±5 minutes, or a declared 30-minute uncertainty window if the bootstrap shows instability.

**Loop 3 — House system consensus check (runs once, can trigger Loop 1 restart)**
As astrologer: this is the check I'd never skip. The same 5-minute candidate time is run through all four house systems — Placidus, Koch, Whole Sign, and Regiomontanus. For each system, the entire scoring run is re-executed with that system's house cusps. Consensus means 3 of 4 systems converge on the same 5-minute window. If only 2 agree, the output confidence is reduced. If all 4 give different results, the system does not emit a rectified time — it reports that the problem is underdetermined and explains which systems agree on what. This is the most important honesty check in the entire workflow, and it's the one most automated systems skip because it's computationally expensive and produces inconvenient non-answers.

---

### Cross-validation pass

The held-out event set — events deliberately excluded from all scoring runs — is now used as a test set. The rectified time's chart is scored against these events. If the technique hit rate on held-out events is within 80% of the hit rate on training events, the result is confirmed. If the held-out rate is much lower, it suggests overfitting: the system found a time that explains the training events but doesn't generalize. In this case the result is flagged as provisional and a wider window is reported.

Physical appearance is checked as a weak disqualifier only: if the rectified chart produces a rising sign whose classical physical description is strongly inconsistent with the native's documented physical characteristics, this is noted in the evidence report but does not by itself override a technically strong result. As astrologer I know this check is too noisy to be a primary filter. As data scientist I know its false negative and false positive rates are both too high to be useful as a binary decision.

---

### The evidence ledger — a developer-first commitment

Every rectification produces a structured JSON document, not just a birth time. For each life event: the event date, the technique applied, the natal point activated, the orb at the time of the event, the score awarded, and whether the event was in the training or held-out set. This makes the result falsifiable. A future researcher who finds a new hard-dated event for the same native can add it to the ledger and see immediately whether it confirms or challenges the rectified time. This is the difference between a result and an argument.

---

### The uncertainty floor — a commitment I'd hard-code

If after Loop 2 the top candidate's posterior probability is less than 40% and no single 5-minute window is clearly isolated, the system emits a 30-minute window with an explicit uncertainty flag, not a falsely precise time. If the available events are fewer than 8 hard-dated anchor events, the output is capped at a 30-minute precision regardless of what the scorer produces. The temptation in any automated system is to converge because the algorithm terminates — not because the data actually support termination. Hard-coding the uncertainty floor in the output layer, as a policy that no scoring result can override, is the only way to prevent this.

The system that results from all three roles working together is, fundamentally, a structured argument machine: it generates the strongest possible case for a specific birth time, then tries to break it, then reports how much of it survived. The astrologer decides what counts as evidence. The data scientist decides how to weight and combine it. The developer makes sure the result is reproducible, auditable, and honest about what it doesn't know.
